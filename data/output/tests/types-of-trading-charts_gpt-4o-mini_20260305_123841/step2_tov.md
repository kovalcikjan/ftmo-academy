## Types of Trading Charts – Candlestick Charts, Renko Charts, Heikin Ashi Candles

The methods for displaying price data are extensive. Consequently, no two traders interpret charts in precisely the same way. While most traders rely on candlestick charts, which we discussed in the previous video, there are deeper nuances within this category. Additionally, we will explore alternative graphical representations, including bar charts, Renko charts, and Heikin Ashi candles.

### Candlestick Charts

We previously covered Japanese candlesticks, so here’s a brief recap. They are the most widely used method for technical analysis. Each candlestick consists of a body and wicks. A bullish candlestick forms when the closing price exceeds the opening price, while a bearish candlestick occurs when the closing price falls below the opening price. The wicks indicate the highest and lowest prices the market traded during the period.

![](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-25.jpg)

The image below shows two candlestick charts side by side on the FTMO cTrader platform.

![](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/timeframevsrange.png)

Despite their similar appearance, a key difference exists. The chart on the left is time-based, while the chart on the right is not. The periodicity of the chart is an essential feature that traders can control. Time-based periodicity is the most common, with trading platforms allowing settings for minutes, hours, days, weeks, and even seconds. Traders frequently utilize 1-minute, 5-minute, 15-minute, 30-minute, 60-minute, 4-hour, daily, weekly, and monthly charts. While you can create charts with any timeframe, using commonly watched periods enhances the reliability of price patterns as many traders observe them. Utilizing less conventional timeframes, such as a 40-minute or 3-hour chart, may lead to patterns that carry less significance since fewer traders monitor them. Non-time-based charts, while less common, can still provide valuable insights. Tick charts and range charts are two popular non-time-based options available on the FTMO cTrader platform. In tick charts, one tick indicates a single transaction, or a minimal price increment. For example, in the EURUSD market, quoted in five decimal places, one tick equals 0.00001 or 1 pipette. In range charts, each new bar forms once the price movement between its high and low reaches a predetermined range, resulting in bars with consistent range that close at either high or low. 

Using tick and range charts can reduce noise and clarify trends by eliminating periods of inactivity in the market. This is evident when comparing a DAX four-hour chart with a 100-range chart in cTrader. The uptrends and downtrends appear much cleaner on the range chart, as the time factor has been removed and focus is placed solely on price rotations.

### Bar Charts

The primary distinction between candlestick charts and bar charts lies in their visual representation.

![](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/Snimek-obrazovky-2023-07-18-115043.png)

Bar charts, also known as OHLC charts, consist of vertical bars with two notches that indicate the opening and closing prices. For novice traders, bar charts may be less intuitive for identifying candlestick patterns compared to Japanese candlesticks. However, they can provide a clearer representation for marking support and resistance levels.

### Renko Charts

Renko charts, like range and tick charts, eliminate the influence of time and transform the visual presentation of the chart. Instead of candlesticks, Renko charts utilize bricks. The term "Renko" derives from the Japanese word "renga," meaning brick.

![](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/Snimek-obrazovky-2023-07-18-115503.png)

A new brick forms on a Renko chart when the market moves a specified number of pips away from the previous brick. For instance, if one brick represents a movement of 5 pips, no new bricks will be drawn until the market moves that distance in one direction from the last brick's close. Renko charts effectively filter out noise and assist in identifying support and resistance areas.

### Heikin Ashi Candles

Heikin Ashi candles represent another chart type originating from Japan, translating to "average bar." Although the name is not critical, understanding the calculation method is beneficial. Heikin Ashi candles are constructed similarly to traditional candlestick charts, but with different calculations. You can configure a Heikin Ashi chart based on time, range, or tick.

![](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/Snimek-obrazovky-2023-07-18-115656.png)

Heikin Ashi candles smooth price movements, facilitating a clearer approach to trend following. However, this smoothing can also obscure valuable information, as it may overly simplify the chart. Still, these candles can be effective tools for trade entries and management.

### Conclusion

To maximize your trading effectiveness, experiment with various chart types and settings to determine what best suits your strategy. While traditional time-based candlestick charts are the most widely used globally, tick charts, range charts, Renko charts, and Heikin Ashi candles can enhance your trading insights and strategies.
