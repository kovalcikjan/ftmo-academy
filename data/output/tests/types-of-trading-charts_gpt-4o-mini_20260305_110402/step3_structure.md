# Types of Trading Charts

Understanding market fluctuations and volatility in real-time is a critical skill for any trader. Various methods exist to display data on charts, from traditional candlestick charts to Renko charts and Heikin Ashi candles. This lesson outlines the specifics of these charts and their differences.

## Types of Trading Charts

The options for displaying data on price charts are extensive. This diversity means no two traders analyze the market in precisely the same way. While most traders utilize candlestick charts, as discussed in the previous video, there are additional depths to explore. In addition to candlestick charts, we will also examine alternative graphical representations such as bar charts, Renko charts, and Heikin Ashi candles.

### Candlestick Charts

Having covered Japanese candlesticks in the previous lesson, we will provide a brief recap. They are the most widely used method for charting and conducting technical analysis. The structure of a candlestick includes the body and the wick. A bullish candlestick occurs when the candle closes above its opening price, while a bearish candlestick closes below the opening price. The wicks indicate the highest and lowest trading points within the timeframe.

![Candlestick Chart](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-25.jpg)

The image below shows two candlestick charts side by side in the FTMO cTrader platform.

![Candlestick Comparison](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/timeframevsrange.png)

While they appear similar, a significant difference exists between them. The chart on the left is time-based, while the chart on the right is not. The periodicity of the chart is a key aspect you can control when setting up a candlestick chart. Time periodicity is the most common choice among traders. Most trading platforms allow chart setups based on minutes, hours, days, weeks, and seconds. The most prevalent charts are 1-minute, 5-minute, 15M, 30M, 60M, 4-hour, daily, weekly, and monthly. 

> **Tip:** Although you can configure your chart settings as desired, using popular timeframes is beneficial since many traders monitor them.

A less common timeframe, such as a 40-minute or 3-hour chart, may yield less impactful candlestick patterns since fewer traders observe them. Non-time-based charts, while less common, can still provide valuable insights. In the FTMO cTrader platform, traders can utilize tick and range charts; these are among the most popular non-time-based options. In a tick chart, one tick represents a single transaction. Essentially, a tick occurs when the market fluctuates by the smallest price increment.

For example, considering EURUSD, quoted in five decimal places, one tick equals 0.00001 or 1 pipette. In a range bar chart, each bar closes once the range between its high and low equals the selected range. Consequently, every bar maintains the same range and closes either at its high or low. Utilizing range or tick charts can help eliminate noise and present trends more clearly by excluding periods when the market is stagnant and range-bound. This distinction is evident when comparing a DAX four-hour chart with a 100 range chart in cTrader. The uptrend and downtrend appear much cleaner on the range chart, as the time factor has been removed, allowing a focus on price rotations.

### Bar Charts

The only difference between candlestick charts and bar charts lies in their visual representation.

![Bar Chart](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/Snimek-obrazovky-2023-07-18-115043.png)

Bar charts, also known as OHLC charts, are depicted as vertical bars with two notches indicating the open and close of the bar. Compared to Japanese candlesticks, bar charts can be less intuitive for new traders regarding candlestick patterns. However, they may provide a clearer picture for identifying support and resistance levels.

### Renko Charts

Renko charts share similarities with range and tick charts. They eliminate the time factor from trading and fundamentally alter the visual representation of the chart. Instead of candlesticks, you will observe bricks. The term "Renko" originates from the Japanese word "renga," meaning brick.

![Renko Chart](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/Snimek-obrazovky-2023-07-18-115503.png)

A new brick is printed on the Renko chart when the market moves more than the designated brick size from the previous brick. For instance, if one brick represents a movement of 5 pips, no additional bricks will be drawn until the market moves 5 pips in one direction from the last brick's close. Renko charts are effective for filtering out noise and identifying support and resistance areas.

### Heikin Ashi Candles

Heikin Ashi candles represent another charting method originating from Japan. The term translates to "average bar." While this terminology is not crucial for trading, understanding how Heikin Ashi candles are calculated can be beneficial. Heikin Ashi candles are constructed similarly to candlesticks but utilize a distinct calculation method. You can set up a Heikin Ashi chart based on time, range, or tick.

![Heikin Ashi Chart](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/Snimek-obrazovky-2023-07-18-115656.png)

Heikin Ashi filters out price movements, making trend-following easier. The trade-off is that you may miss some valuable information, as the chart's smoothing can obscure certain signals. Nevertheless, it is a useful tool for both entry points and trade management.

### Conclusion

To effectively navigate different trading environments, experiment with various charts and settings to determine what best suits your trading style. Regular time-based candlestick charts remain the most popular choice among traders globally. However, incorporating tick charts, range charts, Renko charts, or Heikin Ashi candles into your strategy can enhance your trading approach significantly.
