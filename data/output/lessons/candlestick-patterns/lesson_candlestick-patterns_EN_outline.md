## Differentiation

- **Short navigational overview, not a deep dive.** Competitors publish 3,500–9,000-word exhaustive pattern catalogues. This Lekce acts as a concise map to the chapter and individual pattern articles.
- **One explicit category framework.** The Lekce commits to a single framework — reversal / continuation / indecision — so the reader does not meet three competing schemes across the series. Bullish/bearish are treated as attributes of patterns, not the top-level split.
- **Context over count.** The overview states up front that the same candle shape carries different meaning depending on the surrounding trend. This differentiates from dictionary-style lists and aligns with the Academy "realistic educator" ToV.
- **Honest limitations framing.** One "Keep in Mind" callout on pattern reliability and confirmation, placed early — trains readers to approach the chapter with realistic expectations.
- **Routing, not teaching.** The Lekce deliberately does not explain individual patterns. Explanations live in the chapter (2.3.1.) and the pattern subtopics (2.3.1.x). The Lekce's job is orientation.

## What you will learn in this lesson (H2)

Target keyword: `bullish candlestick patterns`, `bearish candlestick patterns`, `reversal candlestick patterns` — woven naturally into the bullet descriptions.

Key points:
- Opening lead-in sentence (not a heading): tell the reader the chapter ahead covers three families of patterns and why each family matters. The bullets below preview those families.
- Four scannable items, each a single short paragraph or tight bullet (1–2 sentences each): what the category is + why it matters for a trader. No pattern is explained here; each is named and linked.

Bullet list (not H3s — keeps the Lekce compact):

- **Reversal patterns** — signal a potential turn at the end of a trend. Covers single-candle signals (Hammer, Shooting Star, Doji variants) and multi-candle formations (Engulfing, Morning Star, Evening Star, Harami, Tweezer Top/Bottom). These are the patterns most beginners learn first because they mark decision points.
- **Continuation and strength patterns** — signal the trend is likely to persist. Includes Marubozu, Three White Soldiers, and Inside Bar. These help traders stay with a move rather than fade it.
- **Indecision patterns** — signal that neither side has control. Doji and Spinning Top sit here. They rarely trigger a trade on their own but often precede a directional move.
- **Reading candles in context** — the single most important section. The same candle means different things at support, at resistance, or mid-trend. The chapter covers how to interpret shape alongside trend and location.

List format: bullet list with bold lead-in term, then one or two sentences. Each bulleted item links (or placeholder-links) to its corresponding 2.3.1.x article.

Callout: **Keep in Mind** — pattern-based trading has real limitations. A candlestick pattern is a probabilistic signal, not a guarantee; most traders combine patterns with trend context and at least one confirming tool.

Internal link placement: one link per bullet to its underlying pattern article, plus one link to the chapter landing page (2.3.1.).

## Why candlestick patterns matter for traders (H2)

Target keyword: `candlestick patterns for trading`

Key points:
- One paragraph (40–70 words): explain the two practical benefits — speed (a glance shows who is in control of a time period) and structure (patterns give consistent rules for what to look for, replacing instinct). Frame them as a reading layer, not a complete strategy.
- One paragraph (40–70 words): address the realistic expectation. Patterns are one input among several; they work best combined with trend context (already covered in 2.2.) and with a defined risk rule (covered in Part 3). Avoid any language suggesting patterns "predict" price.
