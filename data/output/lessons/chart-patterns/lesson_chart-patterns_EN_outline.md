## Outline

### H1: Chart Patterns

Key point: introduce chart-pattern analysis as one of several analytical lenses traders apply to price charts, and preview the four frameworks covered in this lesson.

---

### Intro (2–3 short paragraphs, ~40–70 words each — before any H2)

Paragraph 1 — What a chart pattern is, in one sentence that defines the term directly (AIO-friendly). Short framing: patterns are recurring shapes in price data that traders study to anticipate likely next moves.

Paragraph 2 — Why they work at all: market participants repeat similar behaviour at similar price levels, and patterns are the visual record of that behaviour. Acknowledge that pattern analysis is one valid approach, not the only one.

Paragraph 3 (optional, can be merged with 2) — Introduce the idea that "chart patterns" in this lesson means more than the classical formations most guides show. The module covers four different frameworks.

---

### H2: What You Will Learn in This Lesson

Short lead-in sentence: the lesson is built around four standalone chapters, each covering a different pattern-based framework.

Bullet list (one bullet per chapter — 1–2 sentences each, covering **what** + **why it matters**):

- **Price formations** (chapter 2.4.1) — classical shapes such as head and shoulders, double tops and bottoms, triangles, wedges, flags, and cup and handle. These are the patterns most trading education starts with, and the foundation every trader sees referenced in market commentary.
- **Pivot points** (chapter 2.4.2) — calculated support and resistance levels derived from the previous session's price data. Traders use them as intraday reference points for entries, exits, and targets.
- **Harmonic patterns** (chapter 2.4.3) — geometric formations (Gartley, Butterfly, Bat, ABCD) defined by specific Fibonacci relationships between swings. A more mechanical approach suited to traders who prefer rule-based setups.
- **Wyckoff methodology** (chapter 2.4.4) — a framework for reading accumulation and distribution phases in market structure. It connects price pattern behaviour to underlying supply and demand rather than treating shapes in isolation.

Callout: **Keep in Mind** — chart-pattern analysis is a valid approach, not a guarantee. Many professional traders combine patterns with volume, support and resistance, and broader context rather than trading patterns in isolation.

---

### H2: Why Chart Patterns Matter for Traders

1 short paragraph: patterns give traders a shared vocabulary and a structured way to describe what price is doing. Recognising them quickly is part of becoming fluent in chart reading.

1 short paragraph: the four frameworks in this lesson cover different time horizons and levels of structure. Price formations and pivots tend to be shorter-term tactical; harmonic and Wyckoff add structural context. Most traders end up using one or two deeply rather than all four.

No callout here (would duplicate the framing above).

---

### Closing paragraph / CTA

Short forward-moving sentence that directs the reader to the first chapter (price formations) and frames the rest of the module as optional depth, not required reading in strict order.
