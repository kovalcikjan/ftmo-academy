# How to Read Trading Charts and Choose a Timeframe (H1)

**Key point:** Charts and timeframes are the two most basic tools of technical analysis. Before you learn patterns, indicators, or strategies, you need to know what a chart is showing you and what timeframe it is showing you. This module covers both.

**Intro (~120 words):**
- One sentence on why this matters: every technical decision starts with reading the right chart at the right timeframe.
- What the reader gets from this page: a map of the module, brief orientation on each topic, links to the full chapter for depth.
- Honest framing: this is an introduction. If you already know the basics of charts and candlesticks, skim ahead and pick the chapter that matches your gap.

## Types of Trading Charts (H2) → 2.1.1.

**Target concept:** Three chart types — line, bar, candlestick.

Key points (~100 words):
- Line chart: connects closing prices — clean overview, no intraday detail.
- Bar chart (OHLC): shows open, high, low, close for each period as a vertical bar.
- Candlestick chart: same OHLC data as bar charts, displayed as a filled or hollow body with wicks. Visually easier to read momentum.
- Most retail and professional charting platforms default to candlesticks; bar charts remain common in futures and older technical analysis literature.

## Anatomy of a Candlestick (OHLC) (H2) → 2.1.2.

**Target concept:** How to read a single candle.

Key points (~100 words):
- A candle represents four price points for its time period: Open, High, Low, Close (OHLC).
- The body is the distance between open and close. The wick (or shadow) shows the full high-low range.
- Bullish candle: close is above open (usually green or hollow). Bearish candle: close is below open (usually red or filled).
- The candle's shape tells you what happened within the period — where price ended compared with where it started, and how far it travelled in between.

## Choosing a Timeframe for Your Trading Style (H2) → 2.1.3.

**Target keyword:** best timeframe for day trading

Key points (~140 words):
- A timeframe is the period each candle represents: one minute (M1), five minutes (M5), one hour (H1), one day (D1), and so on.
- Higher timeframes produce fewer signals and smoother trends. Lower timeframes produce more signals and more noise.
- Four common trading styles match natural timeframe ranges:
  - Scalping: M1–M15
  - Day trading: M15–H4
  - Swing trading: H4–D1
  - Position trading: D1–MN
- The right timeframe depends on how much screen time you have, your personality, and your risk tolerance. Beginners typically start higher (H4 or D1) where noise is lower and signals are clearer.

## Multiple Timeframe Analysis (H2) → 2.1.4.

**Target keyword:** multiple timeframe analysis

Key points (~100 words):
- Most professional traders look at more than one timeframe before entering a trade.
- The common pattern: a higher timeframe for direction and context, a lower timeframe for entry timing.
- Typical pairings include D1 with H4 for swing traders, H4 with M30 for intraday traders, and H1 with M5 for scalpers.
- Using multiple timeframes improves trade selection because it filters out setups that fight the dominant trend on the higher timeframe.

Link out: [Read the full chapter on multiple timeframe analysis →](/lesson/multiple-timeframe-analysis/)

## Trading Sessions and Hours (H2) → 2.1.5.

**Target concept:** Forex session overview and session-to-timeframe relationship.

Key points (~100 words):
- Forex runs 24 hours, five days a week, rotating through three main sessions: Asian (Tokyo), European (London), and US (New York).
- Volatility and liquidity change across sessions. The London–New York overlap produces the highest liquidity for most major pairs.
- Session timing affects which timeframe works best: scalping on M1–M5 rewards peak-liquidity hours; swing trading on D1 is session-agnostic because a single candle spans all three.
- Stock and futures markets have their own session calendars, typically tied to exchange hours in the instrument's home region.
