## What Are Japanese Candlestick Charts?

**Japanese candlesticks** belong to the most popular methods of technical analysis. The technique dates back to the 18th century, when traders at Japanese rice exchanges first began identifying recurring price patterns — marking the earliest foundations of technical trading.

## Why Use Candlestick Patterns?

The key advantage of candlestick patterns is their combination of simplicity and informational value. Learning how to read candlesticks allows you to quickly assess market dynamics: are buyers stronger than sellers, or vice versa? Is the trend likely to continue or reverse? Candlestick shapes reveal **trader psychology** — each pattern reflects the balance of buying and selling pressure during a given period.

## Single Candlestick Patterns

### Long day bullish

The long day bullish candlestick consists of one candle with a long body and short wicks. It signals market strength and is often seen at the beginning of a trend, acting as a breakout candle. Traders use it as confluence rather than a standalone entry or exit signal.

### Long day bearish

The long day bearish candlestick features a large bearish body with small wicks. It signals market weakness and a potential start of a downtrend. Like its bullish counterpart, it provides directional clues but should not be used as a standalone entry or exit signal.

### Short day bullish

The short day bullish candlestick is defined by its minimal length — there is no fixed threshold, so compare it against recent candles in context. It signals that price stayed in a narrow range during the period, with expansion likely to follow. This pattern is not used as a standalone entry signal; its value lies in the broader pattern context.

### Short day bearish

The short day bearish candlestick is the bearish equivalent. Like the bullish version, it carries limited significance on its own — its value comes from the surrounding context and what it reveals about the balance between buyers and sellers.

### Bullish marubozu

**Marubozu** is one of the most straightforward candlestick patterns to identify: a long body with no upper or lower wick. The bullish marubozu signals clear buyer dominance throughout the entire trading period.

Two key contexts for the bullish marubozu:

- In an uptrend: trend continuation is likely.
- In a downtrend: a reversal is likely.

### Bearish marubozu

The bearish marubozu signals seller dominance — the mirror image of the bullish version.

- In a downtrend: continuation is likely.
- In an uptrend: a reversal is likely.

After spotting a marubozu, wait for further confirmation: another candlestick pattern, a key [support or resistance](/lesson/support-and-resistance/) level, or a confirming indicator signal.

### Bullish closing marubozu

The bullish closing marubozu differs from the standard bullish marubozu by a single feature: a small lower wick. Sellers briefly entered after the open, but buyers regained control and drove price higher by the close. In an uptrend, it signals continuation. At a key support level, it signals a potential move higher.

### Bearish closing marubozu

The bearish closing marubozu features a small upper wick and a large bearish body. Buyers briefly pushed price up from the open, but sellers took over and drove it lower by the close. In a downtrend, it signals continuation. At key resistance, it signals a potential move lower.

### Bullish opening marubozu

The bullish opening marubozu has a small upper wick but remains a strongly bullish signal. Buyers dominated from the open; the small upper wick shows that sellers appeared briefly near the top but failed to shift the bullish momentum.

### Bearish opening marubozu

The bearish opening marubozu signals strong seller conviction. Sellers dominated from the open; the small lower wick indicates a brief counter-move by buyers that failed to overcome selling pressure. In a downtrend, it signals continuation; in an uptrend, it indicates a potential reversal.

---

**Marubozu variants at a glance:**

| Pattern | Wick(s) | Signal |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Bullish marubozu | None | Full buyer dominance throughout |
| Bearish marubozu | None | Full seller dominance throughout |
| Bullish closing marubozu | Small lower wick | Buyers overcame sellers by close |
| Bearish closing marubozu | Small upper wick | Sellers overcame buyers by close |
| Bullish opening marubozu | Small upper wick | Buyers dominated from open |
| Bearish opening marubozu | Small lower wick | Sellers dominated from open |

> **Tip:** Marubozu patterns signal strong conviction, but a single candle is rarely sufficient to act on alone. Wait for confirmation from price structure — a nearby [support or resistance](/lesson/support-and-resistance/) level, or a confirming indicator reading — before entering a trade.

### The Spinning Top Candle Pattern

The spinning top is a neutral candlestick pattern: a small body with long upper and lower wicks. The direction of the close matters less than location. At resistance, it signals a potential bearish reversal. At support, it signals a potential bullish reversal.

### The Doji Candlestick Pattern

Like the spinning top, the doji is an indecision pattern — defined by a very small body where the open and close are virtually equal. A doji frequently appears at trend extremes, making it one of the most recognized reversal candlestick patterns in technical analysis. That said, doji patterns can also signal trend continuation in the right context.

Three key doji variations provide more specific signals: the **long-legged doji**, **gravestone doji**, and **dragonfly doji**.

#### Long-legged Doji

The long-legged doji features extended upper and lower wicks relative to its near-zero body — more dramatic than a regular doji. Like the spinning top, it signals strong market indecision and a potential reversal.

#### Gravestone Doji

The gravestone doji is a bearish pattern. Buyers pushed price up during the period, but sellers overcame them and drove it back to the open. In an uptrend, it signals a potential reversal. In a downtrend, it serves as a continuation signal.

#### Dragonfly Doji

The dragonfly doji is a bullish pattern. Sellers pushed price lower during the period, but buyers overcame them and drove it back to the open. In a downtrend, it signals a potential reversal. In an uptrend, it serves as a continuation signal.

---

| Pattern | Shape | Bias | In uptrend | In downtrend |
|---------|-------|------|------------|--------------|
| Long-legged doji | Long upper + lower wicks | Indecision | Potential reversal | Potential reversal |
| Gravestone doji | Long upper wick, no lower | Bearish | Reversal | Continuation |
| Dragonfly doji | Long lower wick, no upper | Bullish | Continuation | Reversal |

> **Keep in Mind:** Some human tendencies can work against you when reading candlestick patterns:
> - Seeing patterns where there aren't any
> - Believing "market lore" without verification
> - Anchoring to the past rather than assessing current conditions
> - Sticking with original pattern targets after market context has changed

## Multi-Candlestick Patterns

### Bullish Engulfing

The **bullish engulfing** is a two-candle pattern. A small bearish candle is followed by a larger bullish candle that completely engulfs the first candle's body.

### Bearish Engulfing

The **bearish engulfing** is the bearish counterpart: a small bullish candle followed by a larger bearish candle that completely engulfs the first candle's body.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Candlestick patterns reveal **trader psychology** — the balance of buying and selling pressure in a given period
- Single candlestick patterns are most effective as **confluence**, not standalone entry or exit signals
- **Marubozu** patterns (no wicks) signal the strongest directional conviction
- **Doji** and **spinning top** signal indecision; their location at support or resistance determines the directional bias
- **Engulfing patterns** require the second candle to fully overlap the first candle's body to be valid
- Always confirm candlestick signals with [support and resistance levels](/lesson/support-and-resistance/) or [technical indicators](/lesson/technical-indicators/) before acting
