Candlestick patterns are one of the most widely used techniques in price
action trading. There are hundreds of different candlestick chart patterns —
you do not need to know all of them. This lesson covers the most common
types of candlesticks used in candlestick trading.

---

## What are Japanese candlesticks?

**Japanese candlestick patterns** originated in 18th-century Japan at the
rice exchange — the earliest known form of technical trading.

![Japanese candlestick anatomy](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-6.jpg)

Each candlestick represents one time period — one minute, one hour, one day,
depending on your chart settings. The **body** shows the distance between
open and close price. The **wicks** (upper and lower) show how far price
moved beyond that range before the period ended.

A **bullish candle** closes higher than it opened — typically displayed in
white or green. A **bearish candle** closes lower than it opened — typically
red or black. This structure is the foundation for every pattern in this
lesson.

---

## Why candlestick patterns matter

The key advantages of candlestick analysis are simplicity and informational
value. Traders learn how to read candlesticks quickly — identifying whether
buyers or sellers are in control, and whether a trend is likely to continue
or reverse.

Candlestick patterns reveal the psychology of market participants. Various
candlestick shapes indicate who has the upper hand. The patterns covered in
this lesson illustrate this directly.

## Single-candle patterns

> **Warning:** Single-candle patterns provide directional context, not
> standalone entry or exit signals. Always confirm with additional patterns,
> [support and resistance levels](/lesson/support-and-resistance/), or
> [technical indicators](/lesson/technical-indicators/).

### Long day bullish

![Long day bullish](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-7.jpg)

The long day bullish candlestick has a long body with short wicks. It signals
market strength — often appearing as a breakout candle at the start of a new
trend. Traders use it as a confluence signal, not as a standalone entry
or exit.

### Long day bearish

![Long day bearish](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-8.jpg)

The long day bearish candlestick displays a large bearish body with small
wicks. It signals weakness and a potential start of a downtrend. Both long
day patterns provide directional clues, but neither should be used as a
standalone entry or exit signal.

### Short day bullish

![Short day bullish](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-9.jpg)

The short day bullish candlestick is a single-candle pattern defined by its
relatively small body. There is no fixed definition of how short the candle
must be — use surrounding price history as a reference. It is not used as
an entry or exit signal. The short day candlestick indicates that price stayed
within a range, with expansion likely to follow. It gains relevance when
found within a larger pattern.

### Short day bearish

![Short day bearish](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-10.jpg)

The short day bearish candlestick is the opposite of its bullish counterpart.
Like the short day bullish, it carries little weight in isolation. Always
evaluate it within the surrounding price action — what it reveals about
buyer and seller strength.

### Bullish marubozu

![Bullish marubozu](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-11.jpg)

The **marubozu** is one of the most straightforward candlestick patterns to
identify. It features a full body with no upper or lower wick. The bullish
marubozu candlestick signals clear buyer dominance — price moved in one
direction from open to close without any pushback.

A bullish marubozu provides two clear trading signals:

- If you spot a bullish marubozu in an [uptrend](/lesson/market-environment-range-vs-trend/),
  trend continuation is likely.
- If a bullish marubozu occurs in a downtrend, a reversal is likely to happen.

### Bearish marubozu

![Bearish marubozu](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-13.jpg)

The bearish marubozu follows the same logic as its bullish counterpart,
reversed:

- If bearish marubozu happens in a downtrend, the trend is likely to continue.
- If bearish marubozu occurs in an uptrend, a reversal is likely to happen.

After identifying a marubozu candle, wait for confirmation from additional
patterns, [support and resistance](/lesson/support-and-resistance/) levels,
or indicators before entering a trade.

### Bullish closing marubozu

![Bullish closing marubozu](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-14.jpg)

The bullish closing marubozu differs from a full bullish marubozu by one
element: a lower wick. This indicates that sellers briefly stepped in after
the open, but buyers were stronger and closed the candle higher. In an
uptrend, it signals continuation. At a key support area, it signals a
potential move higher.

### Bearish closing marubozu

![Bearish closing marubozu](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-15.jpg)

The bearish closing marubozu features a small upper wick and a large bearish
body. Watch for it in two locations: in a downtrend, it signals continuation;
at a key resistance level, it signals a potential move lower.

### Bullish opening marubozu

![Bullish opening marubozu](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-16.jpg)

Despite the small upper wick, the bullish opening marubozu is a strong
bullish signal. Buyers took control from the open — even though sellers
appeared at the top, the bullish bias held.

### Bearish opening marubozu

![Bearish opening marubozu](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-17.jpg)

The bearish opening marubozu signals strong seller conviction. A small lower
wick indicates a brief buying attempt that sellers quickly overcame. In a
downtrend, this pattern signals continuation; in an uptrend, it can signal
a trend reversal.

### The spinning top

![Spinning top](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-18.jpg)

The **spinning top** candlestick pattern is neutral — the direction of the
close (bullish or bearish) matters less than where the candle closes relative
to key levels. It consists of a single candle with a small body and long
wicks, signaling indecision in the market. At a resistance level, the
spinning top is a bearish reversal signal. At a support area, it signals a
bullish reversal.

### The doji candlestick pattern

![Doji](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-19.jpg)

Like the spinning top, the **doji** is an indecision candlestick pattern.
A doji candle has a small body where the open and close are virtually equal.
It is often found at trend tops and bottoms and is considered one of the most
common reversal candlestick patterns. More complex doji patterns can also
appear in trend continuation.

The doji has three key variations: **long-legged doji**, **gravestone doji**,
and **dragonfly doji**.

> **Keep in Mind:** Some human tendencies can work against you when reading
> candlestick patterns:
> - Seeing patterns where there are none
> - Sticking with an original signal bias after market conditions have changed
> - Looking at what happened rather than what is likely to happen next

### Long-legged Doji

![Long-legged doji](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-20.jpg)

The long-legged doji closely resembles the spinning top. Compared to a
regular doji, it is more pronounced — with upper and lower wicks extending
well beyond the body in both directions. Like the spinning top, it signals
indecision and a potential reversal.

### Gravestone Doji

![Gravestone doji](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-21.jpg)

The **gravestone doji** is a bearish candlestick pattern. Buyers pushed price
higher after the open, but sellers overcame them and drove price back to the
open level. In an uptrend, it signals a potential bearish reversal. In a
downtrend, it can serve as a continuation signal.

### Dragonfly Doji

![Dragonfly doji](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-22.jpg)

The **dragonfly doji** is a bullish candlestick pattern. Sellers pushed price
lower after the open, but buyers overcame them and drove price back to the
open level. In a downtrend, it signals a potential bullish reversal. In an
uptrend, it can serve as a continuation signal.

## Two-candle patterns

Engulfing candlestick patterns are the most common two-candle reversal
signals in price action trading.

### Bullish engulfing

![Bullish engulfing](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-23.jpg)

The **bullish engulfing** forms with a small bearish candle on the left and
a larger bullish candle on the right. The bullish candle must completely
engulf the body of the previous candle.

### Bearish engulfing

![Bearish engulfing](https://academy.ftmo.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/c-24.jpg)

The **bearish engulfing** forms with a small bullish candle on the left and
a larger bearish candle on the right. The bearish candle must completely
engulf the body of the previous candle.

---

| Pattern | Candles | Signal | Context |
|---------|---------|--------|---------|
| Long day bullish | 1 | Bullish | Trend start, confluence |
| Long day bearish | 1 | Bearish | Trend start, confluence |
| Short day bullish / bearish | 1 | Neutral | Range, part of larger pattern |
| Marubozu (all variants) | 1 | Bullish / Bearish | Trend continuation or reversal |
| Spinning top | 1 | Neutral | Key support / resistance levels |
| Doji (all variants) | 1 | Neutral / Reversal | Trend tops and bottoms |
| Bullish engulfing | 2 | Bullish | Reversal signal |
| Bearish engulfing | 2 | Bearish | Reversal signal |

## Key Takeaways

- Candlestick patterns reveal market psychology — they show whether buyers
  or sellers are in control at any given moment.
- Single-candle patterns provide directional context. Use them as confluence,
  not as standalone entry or exit signals.
- **Marubozu** candles signal the strongest conviction. Wait for confirmation
  from support/resistance or indicators before acting.
- **Reversal candlestick patterns** — especially doji and spinning top —
  carry the most weight at key support and resistance levels.
- **Engulfing patterns** are two-candle reversal signals — the second candle
  must completely engulf the body of the first.
