Japanese Candlestick Patterns

Candlestick patterns are one of the most widely used techniques in price action trading. There are hundreds of different candlestick chart patterns — you do not need to know all of them. This lesson covers the most common types of candlesticks used in candlestick trading.

What are Japanese candlesticks?

Japanese candlestick patterns are among the most widely used tools in technical analysis. Candlestick pattern analysis originated in 18th-century Japan at the rice exchange — the earliest known form of technical trading.

Why candlestick patterns matter

The key advantages of candlestick analysis are simplicity and informational value. Traders learn how to read candlesticks quickly — identifying whether buyers or sellers are in control, and whether a trend is likely to continue or reverse.

Candlestick patterns reveal the psychology of market participants. Various candlestick shapes indicate who has the upper hand. The patterns covered in this lesson illustrate this directly.

Single-candle patterns

Warning Single-candle patterns provide directional context, not standalone entry or exit signals. Always confirm with additional patterns, support and resistance levels, or technical indicators.

Long day bullish

The long day bullish candlestick has a long body with short wicks. It signals market strength — often appearing as a breakout candle at the start of a new trend. Traders use it as a confluence signal, not as a standalone entry or exit.

Long day bearish

The long day bearish candlestick displays a large bearish body with small wicks. It signals weakness and a potential start of a downtrend. Both long day patterns provide directional clues, but neither should be used as a standalone entry or exit signal.

Short day bullish

The short day bullish candlestick is a single-candle pattern defined by its relatively small body. There is no fixed definition of how short the candle must be — use surrounding price history as a reference. It is not used as an entry or exit signal. The short day candlestick indicates that price stayed within a range, with expansion likely to follow. It gains relevance when found within a larger pattern.

Short day bearish

The short day bearish candlestick is the opposite of its bullish counterpart. Like the short day bullish, it carries little weight in isolation. Always evaluate it within a larger context, considering what buyers and sellers are attempting to achieve.

Bullish marubozu

The marubozu is one of the most straightforward candlestick patterns to identify. It features a full body with no upper or lower wick. The bullish marubozu candlestick signals clear buyer dominance — price moved in one direction from open to close without any pushback.

A bullish marubozu provides two clear trading signals:

  • If you spot a bullish marubozu in an uptrend, trend continuation is likely.
  • If a bullish marubozu occurs in a downtrend, a reversal is likely to happen.

Bearish marubozu

The bearish marubozu follows the same logic as its bullish counterpart, reversed:

  • If bearish marubozu happens in a downtrend, the trend is likely to continue.
  • If bearish marubozu occurs in an uptrend, a reversal is likely to happen.
Tip After identifying a marubozu candle, wait for further confirmation — from additional candlestick patterns, support and resistance levels, or indicators — before entering a trade.

Bullish closing marubozu

The bullish closing marubozu differs from a full bullish marubozu by one element: a lower wick. This indicates that sellers briefly stepped in after the open, but buyers were stronger and closed the candle higher. In an uptrend, it signals continuation. At a key support area, it signals a potential move higher.

Bearish closing marubozu

The bearish closing marubozu features a small upper wick and a large bearish body. Watch for it in two locations: in a downtrend, it signals continuation; at a key resistance level, it signals a potential move lower.

Bullish opening marubozu

Despite the small upper wick, the bullish opening marubozu is a strong bullish signal. Buyers took control from the open — even though sellers appeared at the top, the bullish bias held.

Bearish opening marubozu

The bearish opening marubozu signals strong seller conviction. A small lower wick indicates a brief buying attempt that sellers quickly overcame. In a downtrend, this pattern signals continuation; in an uptrend, it can signal a trend reversal.

The spinning top

The spinning top candlestick pattern is neutral — the direction of the close (bullish or bearish) matters less than where the candle closes relative to key levels. It consists of a single candle with a small body and long wicks, signaling indecision in the market. At a resistance level, the spinning top is a bearish reversal signal. At a support area, it signals a bullish reversal.

The doji candlestick pattern

Like the spinning top, the doji is an indecision candlestick pattern. A doji candle has a small body where the open and close are virtually equal. It is often found at trend tops and bottoms and is considered one of the most common reversal candlestick patterns. More complex doji patterns can also appear in trend continuation.

The doji has three key variations: long-legged doji, gravestone doji, and dragonfly doji.

Keep in Mind Some human tendencies can work against you when reading candlestick patterns:
  • Seeing patterns where there are none
  • Sticking with an original signal bias after market conditions have changed
  • Looking at what happened rather than what is likely to happen next

Long-legged Doji

The long-legged doji closely resembles the spinning top. Compared to a regular doji, it is more pronounced — with upper and lower wicks extending well beyond the body in both directions. Like the spinning top, it signals indecision and a potential reversal.

Gravestone Doji

The gravestone doji is a bearish candlestick pattern. Buyers pushed price higher after the open, but sellers overcame them and drove price back to the open level. In an uptrend, it signals a potential bearish reversal. In a downtrend, it can serve as a continuation signal.

Dragonfly Doji

The dragonfly doji is a bullish candlestick pattern. Sellers pushed price lower after the open, but buyers overcame them and drove price back to the open level. In a downtrend, it signals a potential bullish reversal. In an uptrend, it can serve as a continuation signal.

Two-candle patterns

Engulfing candlestick patterns are the most common two-candle reversal signals in price action trading.

Bullish engulfing

The bullish engulfing forms with a small bearish candle on the left and a larger bullish candle on the right. The bullish candle must completely engulf the body of the previous candle.

Bearish engulfing

The bearish engulfing forms with a small bullish candle on the left and a larger bearish candle on the right. The bearish candle must completely engulf the body of the previous candle.

Key Takeaways

  • Candlestick patterns reveal market psychology — they show whether buyers or sellers are in control at any given moment.
  • Single-candle patterns provide directional context. Use them as confluence, not as standalone entry or exit signals.
  • Marubozu candles signal the strongest conviction. Wait for confirmation from support/resistance or indicators before acting.
  • Reversal candlestick patterns — especially doji and spinning top — carry the most weight at key support and resistance levels.
  • Engulfing patterns are two-candle reversal signals — the second candle must completely engulf the body of the first.
