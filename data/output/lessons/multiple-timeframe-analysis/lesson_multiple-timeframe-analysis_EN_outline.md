## Outline

# Multiple Timeframe Analysis (H1)

Key point: Analyzing the same instrument across different timeframes filters noise, confirms setups, and improves entry timing.

---

## Why Traders Use Multiple Timeframes (H2)

Key points:
- Single-timeframe trading creates blind spots — a bullish signal on M15 means nothing if D1 is in a downtrend
- MTF analysis provides context (higher TF), setup identification (middle TF), and precise entries (lower TF)
- Reduces false signals by requiring confluence across timeframes

Callout: Important — "A trade that looks perfect on one timeframe can be a trap on another. Multiple timeframe analysis eliminates this blind spot."

---

## The Top-Down Approach (H2)

Target keyword: top down analysis trading

Key points:
- Always start from higher timeframe and work down — never bottom-up
- Higher TF establishes directional bias (trend direction, key levels)
- Lower TF provides entry timing and stop placement
- Bottom-up danger: finding a signal first, then rationalizing it on higher TFs (confirmation bias)

Table: Top-Down vs. Bottom-Up comparison (approach, risk, common outcome)

Callout: Keep in Mind — Confirmation bias risk when starting from lower timeframes.

### The Three-Tier Framework (H3)

- Tier 1 — Structure timeframe: overall trend direction and major S/R levels
- Tier 2 — Setup timeframe: pattern recognition, setup identification
- Tier 3 — Entry timeframe: precise entry, stop loss placement, trigger confirmation

---

## Choosing the Right Timeframe Combinations (H2)

Target keyword: day trading time frames

Key points:
- Rule of thumb: 4x to 6x ratio between adjacent timeframes
- Too close = redundant information; too far apart = gaps in analysis
- Combination depends on trading style and holding period

Table: Timeframe Combinations by Trading Style

| Trading Style | Structure TF | Setup TF | Entry TF | Typical Hold |
|---------------|-------------|----------|----------|-------------|
| Position | Monthly | Weekly | Daily | Weeks-months |
| Swing | Weekly | Daily | H4 | Days-weeks |
| Day trading | Daily | H1 | M15 | Hours |
| Scalping | H4 | M15 | M5/M1 | Minutes |

Callout: Tip — "Pick one combination and master it. Switching between timeframe sets introduces inconsistency."

---

## How to Read Multiple Timeframes: Step by Step (H2)

Key points:
- Step 1: Identify trend on structure TF (higher highs/higher lows or the opposite)
- Step 2: Mark key levels on structure TF (support, resistance, supply/demand zones)
- Step 3: Move to setup TF — look for patterns that align with the higher TF bias
- Step 4: Drop to entry TF — wait for a trigger (candlestick pattern, indicator signal, level test)
- Step 5: Place stop loss based on entry TF structure, target based on setup TF levels

Callout: Note — "Levels drawn on higher timeframes carry more weight. A support level on the daily chart is more significant than one on the 15-minute chart."

---

## When Timeframes Conflict (H2)

Key points:
- Conflicting signals are common — e.g., D1 bullish but H4 showing bearish reversal pattern
- Decision framework: higher timeframe wins the directional bias, always
- Three options when conflict exists: (1) wait for alignment, (2) reduce position size, (3) stand aside entirely
- Forcing trades against the higher TF bias is the most common MTF mistake

Callout: Warning — "Trading against the higher timeframe trend is not a strategy — it is gambling with extra steps. When in doubt, stand aside."

---

## Multiple Timeframe Analysis and Risk Management (H2)

Key points:
- Stop loss placement informed by entry TF structure (below/above the nearest level)
- Target setting informed by setup TF levels (next major S/R zone)
- Position sizing unchanged — risk % stays constant regardless of timeframe
- Connection to FTMO: MTF analysis helps avoid impulsive entries that lead to max daily loss breaches

Callout: Tip — "Use the entry timeframe for stop placement and the setup timeframe for targets. This creates favorable risk-to-reward ratios naturally."

---

## Common Mistakes (H2)

Key points:
- Starting analysis bottom-up (confirmation bias trap)
- Using too many timeframes — analysis paralysis (stick to 2-3)
- Ignoring the higher TF bias when lower TF shows a "perfect" setup
- Switching timeframe combinations mid-trade
- Over-optimizing entry on the lowest TF and missing the move
