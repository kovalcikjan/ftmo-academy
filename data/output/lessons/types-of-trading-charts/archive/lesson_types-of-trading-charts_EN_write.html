Types of Trading Charts: Line, Bar, and Candlestick

Every trading decision starts with reading a chart. Before analyzing patterns, placing indicators, or identifying support levels, understand what the chart in front of you is actually showing — and whether it's the right tool for the job.

There are three core trading chart types: line, bar, and candlestick. Each represents the same underlying price data differently. Choosing the right one affects what you see, what you miss, and how fast you can act.

What a Trading Chart Shows

A chart maps price against time. The vertical axis (Y) shows price levels. The horizontal axis (X) shows time. Every data point, bar, or candle represents one time period — one minute, one hour, one day — depending on the timeframe you select.

What looks like a simple visual is actually a compression of thousands of individual transactions. Every trade executed during that period gets absorbed into a single data structure: the open (first price), high (highest price reached), low (lowest price reached), and close (last price). These four values — OHLC — are the raw material every chart type works with.

The difference between chart types is not what data exists. It's how much of it gets displayed, and in what form.

Line Chart

A line chart plots only the closing price of each period and connects those points with a continuous line. Open, high, and low are ignored entirely.

That sounds like a limitation — and it is, for certain tasks. But it's also the line chart's strength. Strip away three quarters of the data and what remains is the clearest possible view of price direction over time. Noise disappears. The trend becomes immediately readable.

When to use a line chart:

  • Higher timeframe analysis where intrabar movement is irrelevant
  • Comparing two or more assets on the same chart
  • Reading economic data (GDP, employment figures) where only closing values exist
Tip Use a line chart to establish trend direction on the higher timeframe. Switch to a candlestick chart when it's time to find an entry.

Bar Chart (OHLC)

A bar chart — also called an OHLC chart — displays all four data points for each period in a single vertical bar.

Here's how to read it:

  • The top of the vertical line = period high
  • The bottom of the vertical line = period low
  • The short horizontal tick on the left = opening price
  • The short horizontal tick on the right = closing price

The height of the bar tells you how much price moved during that period. A tall bar means significant volatility — buyers and sellers were fighting for ground. A short bar means tight, compressed price action.

Bar charts contain the same information as candlestick charts. The visual encoding is just less intuitive. There's no color coding, no filled body — sentiment doesn't jump off the screen the way it does with candlesticks. This is why bar charts, while still used in futures and commodities markets, lost ground to candlesticks as the default for active traders.

Keep in Mind Bar and candlestick charts show identical data. The difference is reading speed. On a bar chart, identifying bullish or bearish sentiment requires conscious comparison of the open/close ticks. On a candlestick chart, color does that work instantly.

Candlestick Chart

The candlestick chart is the industry standard for active trading. It displays the same OHLC data as the bar chart, but encodes it in a format built for faster reading.

Each candle has two parts:

  • Body — the filled rectangle between the open and close prices
  • Wicks (also called shadows) — the thin lines above and below the body, showing the high and low extremes

Color tells you direction at a glance:

  • Green or white body — close is above the open (bullish period)
  • Red or black body — close is below the open (bearish period)

Japanese candlesticks became the standard for one reason: color coding makes sentiment readable in under a second. On a bar chart, you need to locate the open and close ticks and compare them consciously. On a candlestick chart, a green candle with a large body communicates "buyers were in control" without any calculation.

How to Read a Candlestick

Reading a single candle takes three steps:

  1. Body — What color is it, and how large is it? A large body means strong directional conviction. A small body means the open and close ended near the same level — indecision, no clear winner between buyers and sellers.
  2. Wicks — Where did price go that it couldn't hold? A long lower wick means sellers pushed price down, but buyers rejected that level and drove it back up. That rejection is meaningful — it often marks a support level.
  3. Context — One candle in isolation means little. The same candle after a prolonged downtrend means something very different than the same candle mid-range. Always read the candle in sequence.
Example A green candle with a large body and short wicks = buyers controlled the entire period from open to close. No rejection, no hesitation. That's a high-conviction bullish candle.

Individual candlestick patterns — doji, hammer, engulfing — are covered in the Chart Patterns lesson.

Which Chart Type Should You Use?

Chart Type Data Shown Best For Limitation
Line Close only Trend context, asset comparison No intrabar information
Bar (OHLC) Open, High, Low, Close Full data, minimalist display Slower sentiment reading
Candlestick Open, High, Low, Close Active trading, pattern recognition Can create noise on very short timeframes

The practical answer for most traders:

Use a line chart on higher timeframes to read the trend. Use a candlestick chart on your execution timeframe to read price action and make decisions.

Bar charts remain valid — some traders prefer the cleaner aesthetic — but they offer no informational advantage over candlesticks. The data is identical; the visual encoding is less efficient.

In an FTMO Challenge environment, where decisions need to be precise and fast, candlestick charts give you the fastest sentiment read at the moments that matter. A large red candle with no lower wick communicates "sellers are in control, do not fight this" in a fraction of a second. That speed of interpretation matters when managing positions near drawdown limits — slower reads cost you reaction time at the worst possible moment.

Note No chart type creates an edge by itself. The edge comes from reading price consistently, in context, across timeframes. Start with candlesticks — they're the industry standard for a reason — and build from there.
